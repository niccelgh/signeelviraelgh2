Aims and Purpose
The purpose of this project is to learn critical and interpretive digitization for preservation and access,
digitization and machine/human-access project management and execution. Within this we the purpose
was to analyze the materials and the steps of the process itself, and learn to make informed critical,
interpretive, technological, and descriptive choices that suit the subject matter, project purpose,
intended audience, and lens of the creator.
The purpose of the project is to digitally preserve and present the photo albums of Signe Elvira Elgh b.
1900 in Molkom, Sweden; to provide the communities of Molkom, Loka, Grythyttan, Kristinehamn with
an archival record of its inhabitants from the early 1900’s; to preserve the visual history of these
communities; and to demonstrate critical and interpretive digitization best practices.
The aim is to digitally preserve through digital scanning; to create rich metadata for the photographs; to
create machine-readable metadata; to make the digitizations and metadata findable and accessible to
machines and humans; to present the digital reproductions and metadata in a critical, interpretive, and
aesthetically thoughtful way for human perusal.
Background
On the author/creator
Signe Elvira Elgh was born into a family that managed Loka Brunn. She later became a hairdresser and
had her own hair salon. Ever the forward-thinking woman she had a child and raised him alone, self-
archived for posterity because she knew her life and experiences as an unmarried woman mattered,
keeping photo memories of her life experiences and loved ones; and recording an oral herstory before
her death hoping that it would share her life and times with her two grandchildren.
On the photo album
Vira had several photo albums and a collection of photos. This particular album is one of Elvira Elgh’s
schoolmates (and occasional friend and relative) from Kristinehamns Praktiska-skola from 1917-1918.
The album is [description here].
On acquiring the album
This album was given to Per Ingemar Elgh upon his mother Signe Elvira Elgh’s death in 1988. Per Elgh
kept them until his death in 2014. They were then passed to his son Per Mathias Elgh and his then-wife
Nicole Elgh in 2015. Nicole initially requested the photos while searching for photos to create Per
Ingemar Elgh’s funeral program.
On the photos
A number of the photos are graduation photos but it is not known if they are Gymnasium graduation
photos or praktska-skola graduation photos. Furthermore, only some of the photos are inscribed with
identifying information. Some may be inscribed on the back but there was concern that removing the
photos may damage the fragile album interior.
At least photo studios c. 1912-1920 are represented here: Systrana Hallberg of Kristinehamn, Johanna
Lundback of Grythyttehed and Loka, Wiborgh of Kristinehamn, unknown (but currently searching for
recognition), Ing. ? Kungliga Hoffotografaren of xx, and xx of Linkoping.

Relevance
This project has relevance to the reparative LIS community; the LGBTQ for work that has been done by
non-binary archivist and lesbians, and for its representation-is-not-gender approach to photo
description; to the feminist community for representation of a single mother self-archiving; to the local
Swedish community and historical societies; to genealogists and family historians; to the Swedish
photography community and critical photo metadata community.
The following research papers, archives, projects, and initiatives as mentioned in the previous section
confirm that relevance:
Homosaurus, LGBTQ reparative metadata/archival reparations; Lesbian Herstory Archive;

Legal Aspects & Copyright Status
This was carefully considered as the material was digitized and published in the United States, but the
author, subjects, and original documents will be returned to Sweden. Sweden adheres to the EU’s
copyright laws which are different; while the United States has “death of the creator plus 70 years” for
works created after 1936 (?). This may pertain to: the photo studio/photographer; those depicted in the
photos and their families; the creator of the photo album and her inscriptions. These intellectual
property rights of course pass down to the successors of the creators, and their permission must be
obtained. In my case, I am one of the successors by marriage so I hold in part, the copyright. I give
permission for Fair Use only; all other uses must be cleared with me. However, the families of the
photographers and the families of those depicted in the photos have not been contacted.

Metadata
Embedded metadata
GIMP was used to edit the embedded photo metadata. Types included GIMP Description, IPTC, XMP,
EXIF. Metadata was exported from GIMP to XML. The metadata was also browsed using ExifTool.
Interoperability between IPTC and Dublin Core is currently between Title, Subject/Keywords, Creator,
Rights/Copyright Notice, and Description.
Omeka
Choices for each Omeka field were derived from Dublin Core guidelines from DCMI Metadata terms
https://www.dublincore.org/specifications/dublin-core/dcmi-terms/ .
Descriptions
I got ideas on how to describe the photos from similar materials on DigitaltMuseum as well as
referencing reparative metadata standards.

Encoding
Encoding this project was both daunting and easy. The easy part was exporting the metadata to XML
using controlled vocabulary tags and IPTC standards; and using Omeka’s Dublin Core fields as a standard
for describing the documents for digital asset management purposes. Using GIMP and Omeka’s fields
took the guesswork out of what to describe. The challenge was how to describe it. Research had to be
done on what exactly to enter into each field, from Title (how should this photo be titled?), Author (who
is the author?) to Copyright (who holds the copyright?) Location and Sublocation (whose? Where?) and
most challenging of all, Description. This is where my critical and interpretive approach came into play,
and I had to have some theoretical basis, rather than standardized or legal, for the choices that I made.
Particularly titles and descriptions took a considerable amount of time and research to produce sensitive
reparative descriptive metadata that was also compatible with how similar photographs are described in
the national institutions in Sweden (albeit in English for now).

Methodology & Choices
Materials to digitize
I had initially considered a number of projects, most of them involving traveling to digitize slave ship
logbooks or plantation records, or my father’s architectural drawings which were of interest to the
architecture department at Tuskegee University. Due to my life circumstances, unfortunately, those
were not feasible.
One project I started, (one of several false starts) was of orphanage records which I promptly stopped
after realizing that that information couldn’t be made public. Even working with Elvira’s materials, I had
media that was difficult (at the time) to make accessible to the public. This photo album proved to be
the right size, compact, with a pre-determined theme and order, with some labeling. Digitizing a self-
styled “yearbook” from the self-archiving efforts of an enterprising single parent who identified as a cis
woman in the 1900s-1980's seem to be good subject matter, and paired with my reparative description
goals, seemed relevant to a number of communities.
Sourcing and Legal Rights
As I described, this material was sourced from my child’s father after his father died. He sent it to the
United States after I left Sweden so that I could digitize it, as another family member was interested in
seeing and possessing it. With our family as the copyright holder, the only issue was to understand if I
could make images of the people represented in the photos, public. Most of them were born over 120
years ago, and the photos taken over 100 years ago.
Image Capture
A self-service book scanner at the University of Colorado, Boulder library was easily accessible. The
resolution was not on the level of those used for rare books and the highest level preservation efforts;
nor did I have access to a quality camera and studio setup to photograph the book or pages uniformly.
So the self-service KIC (Knowledge Imaging Center) scanner at the library seemed the best option. It was
very gentle on the book, it captured all of the information that I needed (with the exception of a sharp
image of the metal embellishment on the cover of the album which consistently cause a bright reflection
until I moved it to a distance which it would not capture the sharpest image). I was able to create TIFF,
PNG, and JPEG at resolutions up to 16 megapixels. The positioning of the book, however, was limited to
the book cradle, as was the color background.

Preservation file format
I chose PNGs as preservation file formats, not 16-bit as I had wanted but PNGs are nearly as good as TIFF
when it comes to image quality, especially for primarily digital use. TIFF is the standard when it comes to
print media, but PNG works quite well for digital graphics.
Presentation file format
I chose JPEG (.jpg) as a presentation format due to its image-quality-for-size. I knew that I would be
making a flipbook and uploading more portable versions of these images so JPEG seemed to be the
natural choice for presentation.
Metadata and descriptions
On the metadata schemas and controlled vocabularies
Since I was dealing with photograph metadata, I had to consider embedded and descriptive schemes –
this meant that I was going to work with Exif, IPTC, XMP and also Dublin Core. Other, paid photo editing
software such as Adobe have more robust and collaborative metadata schemas. GIMP also managed to
export my XML metadata with unexpected characters that required me to go into each file and remove
them before the XML was error-free.
On the descriptive choices
The descriptive metadata was done with attention to reparative metadata practices, this time with an
emphasis on gender range and so has avoided gendering those pictured as their self-understood
genders were not known. Instead
The descriptions were assisted by my partner Jesse Pollak, a Dallas-based documentary photographer
with a critical eye for how photos are described. Her own gender fluidity and academic background
documenting the trans sex-work community in Mexico gives her a sensitivity toward gender
presentation and designation in photographs.
Software used
Image editing
Due to budget constraints, I used GIMP for image editing, although I did not have to do much editing
because the KIC stations have a function that allows for you to choose to crop the scans before
sending/downloading them. I should have chosen no cropping, however, because I feel that I lost a bit of
spatial context with the cropping that I would have preferred remain on original scans.
Encoding
Due to budget constraints, free software and online services were used. GIMP, GitHub, and Notepad ++
were my primary software choices.

Omeka seemed a simple and long-term preservation-oriented solution initially but did not fulfill the

Presentation design
Like many others, I felt the flipbook format to be a nice simulation of browsing through this photo
album, especially for my audience who has not spent sufficient time on the web to tire of this sort of
thing. It is reminiscent of the analog era and I thought it fitting for a 100+ year old memory album.
However, finding aesthetic (and free) flipbook solutions was difficult.
On aesthetic choices
I chose a CSS sheet that (I felt) invoked a feel of the early 1910’s to display the images and information
on the images. It is still a work in progress, however, as customizing the CSS to my needs proved a little
more time-consuming than I had expected.

Methodological Considerations
The initial selection journey
This project was a long and arduous decision-making process. It began in 2021 with the acquisition of
the photo books here in the US (sent from Sweden) as well as cassette tapes. Initially, my aim was to
make Elvira’s oral herstory available, but the DCHM course did not have an instructor specializing in
audio recordings and I was discouraged from doing this. I had originally digitized the tapes in 2017 while
in Sweden, using a tape player and audio cable into the desktop PC. The quality of the digitization was
not great. The tapes were also quite fragile. As a result, the digitization was very noisy and the voice
volume was very low, and I needed to process the digitized audio files through noise reduction filters.
Elvira’s grandson and I then attempted to painstakingly translate what she was saying into English but
found it difficult. I then created a TEI transcription of a printed poem called “Minnen av Loka Brunn”
that I would then pair with an album of photos from Loka Brunn, and a few of the paraphrased
translations from the audio tapes. However, the project seemed both too extensive and not cohesive
enough. Over a year later, I chose the Kamrater album as a discrete and cohesive unit that I felt would

Document attributes
Elvira’s photo album lent itself to digitization on a book scanner and even if less of a priority than films
and negatives which degenerate faster, this object could easily be lost to time, poor storage/handling,
and other forms of degradation. Informational content can be adequately captured in digital form, and
the physical formats and condition of the material do represent major impediments. No intermediates
were involved. The project was just large enough to be project-worthy and just small enough to handle
as a solo; it is not complex in variety but diverse enough to make critical comparisons.
Preservation Considerations
The material was put at moderate risk in the digitization process but at less risk than repeated casual
browsing that it may normally encounter as a family heirloom in a private collection. Digital surrogates
will definitely reduce use of the originals offering them protection from handling and a change for
physical conservation. The digital reproduction is not only seen as a means to replace the originals but
as a means to create access to their contents.
Organization and Available Documentation
The material is a photo album with a clear theme and is in a coherent, logically structured order. It is not
paginated by number but the arrangement suggested by placement by the original creator. Is it
complete insofar as the missing photographs were removed by the creator and are likely to be found in
a loose collection of her pictures—but insofar as the decisions she made with the album, yes this is
complete. There is minimal descriptive information inscribed in the album. There is no navigational, or
structural information about the material, such as bibliographic records other than the metadata which I
created.
Intended Uses
I anticipate these resources being used at the same frequency similar content on DigitaltMuseum, or
similar resources via Rotter (a Swedish genealogy site with photos), Riksarkivet pages; Värmland's pages;
Kristinehamns Praktiska-skola Facebook and web pages; and Fotografregistret are accessed. This
information might be gathered from page view or download information from servers. My
understanding of user requirements is that they will want educational, private, journalistic, or civic use
of the original digitized files as well as a place to browse the collection and its metadata. Digitization can
support these uses. Because this collection’s existence wasn’t known outside of family. However, a
relative who is a family historian has requested the photographs. This will resolve limited access that all
family members have to this documentation. Thus, on both counts – the collection coming to be release
and the ability for all family to access its contents as well as material conservation will be significantly
enhanced by digitization. The files and GitHub repository on which they are situated can support a range
of uses such as printing, browsing, and detailed review. There are no issues that I know of around
security or access to the digital files that I can think of. However, access to the original documents would
certainly have been restricted due to their fragility and private possession.

Digital Collection Building
There is added incentive here to demonstrate various approaches to implicit subject matter embedded
in this photo album. Thematic coherence can be along several themes as mentioned in the earlier
section on the Relevance of this project: for reparative photo description, for feminist self-archiving, for
local historical significance, for photo studio records, for institutional histories, for fashion historians,
etc.
There is potential for collaboration from various parties, archives, and websites to enrich the accuracy
and robustness of the metadata, descriptions of this material. There is potential for multi-institutional
cooperation between Varmland, DigitaltMuseum, Loka Brunn, Grythyttan, Kristinehamn Historical
Societies, and Fotografsregistret to identify, contribute, enrich, and utilize this content. Additionally,
there is potential for collaboration with the entities mentioned in the earlier sections on Relevance.
Duplication of Effort
This material has not already been digitized by another trusted source. Only the digital files that I have
produced and will continue to produce possess sufficient quality, documentation, and functionality to
serve my purposes, unless after publication, other sources come forth with negatives, inscribed photos,
and more contextual information about the albums depictions and contents. Fair Use (U.S. Copyright
Act) conditions govern access and use of the files that I have produced; use conditions for any duplicate
digitization files that I have not yet encountered are unknown.
Institutional Capabilities
Since University of Boras has not provided an institutional repository or web server for this project’s
purpose, I have utilized the free GitHub repository and pages as recommended by the instructors of the
DCHM course. GitHub has the requisite technical infrastructure to manage, deliver, and maintain these
digitized materials for the time being. Longer-term preservation potential of GitHub isn’t known but as a
repository site with versioning engine its purpose is to do so. The digital divide notwithstanding, my
principal users (including those who need to access the repository through the library or mobile phone)
have adequate computing and connectivity to make effective use of these materials.
Finances
I can determine the total cost of image acquisition (selection, preparation, capture, indexing, and quality
control) if I am to 1) estimate my fee for the time; 2) record the ideal technical equipment and software
costs. However, no institutional budget was established for this project and the costs needed to be kept
to a minimum. This majorly impacted the technological choices that were made, and the labor hours
were “student-intern” labor hours resulting in class credit rather than money paid. Thus, the cost is
justified for the real and perceived benefits that will accrue from digitization. In a scenario where labor
wages are factored in and the optimal equipment and software are used, I would say that more

Limitations and Future Directions
Limitations
Limitations of this project were primarily financial, additionally, other events and constraints caused this
project to be stopped and started again as well as lack of complete imaging (photography, 16-bit
digitization), extensive coding, and machine readability at this date but will be included in the ongoing
development of this project.
Future Directions
Future directions of this project include ongoing conservation and preservation of the original
object/document in a protected light and climate-controlled storage environment; handling protocol;
maintenance of digital files in formats and on media that favor perpetuity; migration of digital files and
formats as needed for preservation; and maintenance of public access channels to this digital repository
along with the continual improvement of accompanying metadata.
