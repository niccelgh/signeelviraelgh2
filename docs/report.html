Introduction

A brief description of a case study of a photobook that was digitized for cultural heritage purposes Digitizing cultural heritage materials is a multi-step process. It is also a critical and interpretive process.

Aims and Purpose

The purpose of this project is to learn critical and interpretive digitization for preservation and access, digitization and machine/human-access project management and execution. Within this we the purpose was to analyze the materials and the steps of the process itself, and learn to make informed critical, interpretive, technological, and descriptive choices that suit the subject matter, project purpose, intended audience, and lens of the creator.

The purpose of the project is to digitally preserve and present the photo albums of Signe Elvira Elgh b. 1900 in Molkom, Sweden; to provide the communities of Molkom, Loka, Grythyttan, Kristinehamn with an archival record of its inhabitants from the early 1900’s; to preserve the visual history of these communities; and to demonstrate critical and interpretive digitization best practices.

The aim is to digitally preserve through digital scanning; to create rich metadata for the photographs; to create machine-readable metadata; to make the digitizations and metadata findable and accessible to machines and humans; to present the digital reproductions and metadata in a critical, interpretive, and aesthetically thoughtful way for human perusal.



On the author/creator‌

Background

Signe Elvira Elgh was born into a family that managed Loka Brunn. She later became a hairdresser and had her own hair salon. Ever the forward-thinking woman she had a child and raised him alone, self-archived for posterity because she knew her life and experiences as an unmarried woman mattered, keeping photo memories of her life experiences and loved ones; and recording an oral herstory before her death hoping that it would share her life and times with her two grandchildren.

Legal Aspects & Copyright Status

This was carefully considered as the material was digitized and published in the United States, but the

author, subjects, and original documents will be returned to Sweden. Sweden adheres to the EU’s

copyright laws which state x,y,z; while the United States has “death of the creator plus 70 years” for works created after 19xx. This may pertain to: the photo studio/photographer; those depicted in the photos and their families; the creator of the photo album and her inscriptions. These intellectual property rights of course pass down to the successors of the creators, and their permission must be obtained. In my case I am one of the successors by marriage so I hold in part, the copyright. I give permission for Fair Use only; all other uses must be cleared with me. However, the families of the photographers and the families of those depicted in the photos have not been contacted.

Encoding

Encoding this project was both daunting and easy. The easy part was exporting the metadata to XML using controlled vocabulary tags and IPTC standards; and using Omeka’s Dublin Core fields as a standard for describing the documents for digital asset management purposes. Using GIMP and Omeka’s fields took the guesswork out of what to describe. The challenge was how to describe it. Research had to be done on what exactly to enter into each field, from Title (how should this photo be titled?), Author (who is the author?) to Copyright (who holds the copyright?) Location and Sublocation (whose? Where?) and most challenging of all, Description. This is where my critical and interpretive approach came into play, and I had to have some theoretical basis, rather than standardized or legal, for the choices that I made.

Particularly titles and descriptions took a considerable amount of time and research to produce sensitive reparative descriptive metadata that was also compatible with how similar photographs are described in the national institutions in Sweden (albeit in English for now).

Methodological Considerations

The initial selection journey

This project was a long and arduous decision-making process. It began in 2021 with the acquisition of the photo books here in the US (sent from Sweden) as well as cassette tapes. Initially, my aim was to make Elvira’s oral herstory available, but the DCHM course did not have an instructor specializing in audio recordings and I was discouraged from doing this. I had originally digitized the tapes in 2017 while in Sweden, using a tape player and audio cable into the desktop PC. The quality of the digitization was not great. The tapes were also quite fragile. As a result, the digitization was very noisy and the voice volume was very low, and I needed to process the digitized audio files through noise reduction filters.

Elvira’s grandson and I then attempted to painstakingly translate what she was saying into English but found it difficult. I then created a TEI transcription of a printed poem called “Minnen av Loka Brunn” that I would then pair with an album of photos from Loka Brunn, and a few of the paraphrased translations from the audio tapes. However, the project seemed both too extensive and not cohesive enough. Over a year later, I chose the Kamrater album as a discrete and cohesive unit that I felt would help me neatly fulfill the steps of a digitization project while being easily critically analyzable. Many of the 260 project hours were put into false starts and seeing what was and wasn’t viable/feasible.

Document attributes

Elvira’s photo album lent itself to digitization on a book scanner and even if less of a priority than films and negatives which degenerate faster, this object could easily be lost to time, poor storage/handling, and other forms of degradation. Informational content can be adequately captured in digital form, and the physical formats and condition of the material do represent major impediments. No intermediates were involved. The project was just large enough to be project-worthy and just small enough to handle as a solo; it is not complex in variety but diverse enough to make critical comparisons.

Preservation Considerations

The material was put at moderate risk in the digitization process but at less risk than repeated casual browsing that it may normally encounter as a family heirloom in a private collection. Digital surrogates will definitely reduce use of the originals offering them protection from handling and a change for physical conservation. The digital reproduction is not only seen as a means to replace the originals but as a means to create access to their contents.


Organization and Available Documentation

The material is a photo album with a clear theme and is in a coherent, logically structured order. It is not paginated by number but the arrangement suggested by placement by the original creator. Is it complete insofar as the missing photographs were removed by the creator and are likely to be found in a loose collection of her pictures—but insofar as the decisions she made with the album, yes this is complete. There is minimal descriptive information inscribed in the album. There is no navigational, or structural information about the material, such as bibliographic records other than the metadata which I created.

Intended Uses

I anticipate these resources being used at the same frequency similar content on DigitaltMuseum, or similar resources via Rotter (a Swedish genealogy site with photos), Riksarkivet pages; Värmland's pages; Kristinehamns Praktiska-skola Facebook and web pages; and Fotografregistret are accessed. This information might be gathered from page view or download information from servers. My understanding of user requirements is that they will want educational, private, journalistic, or civic use of the original digitized files as well as a place to browse the collection and its metadata. Digitization can support these uses. Because this collection’s existence wasn’t known outside of family. However, a relative who is a family historian has requested the photographs. This will resolve limited access that all family members have to this documentation. Thus, on both counts – the collection coming to be release and the ability for all family to access its contents as well as material conservation will be significantly enhanced by digitization. The files and GitHub repository on which they are situated can support a range of uses such as printing, browsing, and detailed review. There are no issues that I know of around security or access to the digital files that I can think of. However, access to the original documents would certainly have been restricted due to their fragility and private possession.

There is added incentive here to demonstrate various approaches to implicit subject matter embedded in this photo album. Duplication of Effort

This material has not already been digitized by another trusted source. Only the digital files that I have produced and will continue to produce possess sufficient quality, documentation, and functionality to serve my purposes, unless after publication, other sources come forth with negatives, inscribed photos, and more contextual information about the albums depictions and contents. Fair Use (U.S. Copyright Act) conditions govern access and use of the files that I have produced; use conditions for any duplicate digitization files that I have not yet encountered are unknown.

Institutional Capabilities

Since University of Boras has not provided an institutional repository or web server for this project’s purpose, I have utilized the free GitHub repository and pages as recommended by the instructors of the DCHM course. GitHub has the requisite technical infrastructure to manage, deliver, and maintain these digitized materials for the time being. Longer-term preservation potential of GitHub isn’t known but as a repository site with versioning engine its purpose is to do so. The digital divide notwithstanding, my principal users (including those who need to access the repository through the library or mobile phone) have adequate computing and connectivity to make effective use of these materials.

Finances

I can determine the total cost of image acquisition (selection, preparation, capture, indexing, and quality control) if I am to 1) estimate my fee for the time; 2) record the ideal technical equipment and software costs. However, no institutional budget was established for this project and the costs needed to be kept to a minimum. This majorly impacted the technological choices that were made, and the labor hours

were “student-intern” labor hours resulting in class credit rather than money paid. Thus, the cost is justified for the real and perceived benefits that will accrue from digitization. In a scenario where labor wages are factored in and the optimal equipment and software are used, I would say that more justification would have to be established by consulting communities, historical societies, institutions, consortia, and the like. 260 hours at the average archivist rate of $33-40/hr (cite) is a significant amount of money. Add in server costs, software costs, equipment rental or acquisition, conservation supplies, and the like, I would recommend this project as part of a larger initiative for the purposes of offsetting the costs or at least for it to be performed more efficiently in terms of time. The is currently no external institutional commitment, only individual archivist commitment to the ongoing management and preservation of these files on GitHub which has protocols for archiving, preservation, migration, and the like.
